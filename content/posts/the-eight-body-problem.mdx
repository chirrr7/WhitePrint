In orbital mechanics, the two-body problem has a clean solution. Three bodies and it gets complicated. Eight bodies orbiting a single point of gravity — their trajectories warping each other, every move generating a reaction that no single actor can fully predict or control — and you are in the domain of chaos. There is no closed-form answer. There is only the math of who survives the longest in the pull.

The Strait of Hormuz is that point of gravity. Twenty-one miles at its narrowest. Roughly 20.9 million barrels of crude per day in the first half of 2025. Eleven billion cubic feet of liquefied natural gas daily. Nearly a fifth of the world's entire energy supply, threaded through a channel so physically tight that a determined adversary with a relatively small navy can credibly threaten to stop it cold.

On 28 February 2026, eight actors — Iran, the United States, Israel, China, the GCC states, Russia, Europe, and the corridor economies of Turkey and the UAE — entered a problem that none of them can solve alone and that all of them are trying to price correctly. What follows is an attempt to read every ledger simultaneously: who had what before the first strike, what each body is playing for, where the hidden money flows, and what the three plausible endings look like in financial terms.

One methodological note before we begin. Several key variables in this architecture — Iranian shadow-fleet settlement mechanics, the precise economics of yuan-denominated crude carve-outs, the identity of who holds the derivatives optionality on energy volatility — are intentionally opaque. Where exact figures are unavailable from official sources, this analysis constructs bounded estimates from mosaic: sanctions designation records, IMF country data, EIA chokepoint analysis, OFAC filings, trade-finance research, and market reporting. Ranges are presented as ranges. The structural argument does not depend on any single number being exactly right.

## I. The Equation Nobody Solved

The standard framing of this conflict is wrong.

It is not a war for oil. The United States produces enough hydrocarbons to be roughly balanced or a modest net exporter. It does not need Iranian barrels. Israel has none. The framing of resource competition, while not entirely irrelevant, misses what is actually at stake.

This is a war about who pays the risk premium on global order — and specifically, about who controls the financial architecture that sits underneath the physical flow of energy.

To understand why that distinction matters, you need to understand what has been quietly happening for the better part of a decade. Iran, locked out of the formal dollar system by successive rounds of sanctions, built a parallel financial state. The IRGC — the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps — controls an estimated one-third to forty percent of Iran's entire economy through a network of foundations, front companies, and construction conglomerates that operate largely outside parliamentary oversight and, crucially, outside the reach of Western financial surveillance. Through this apparatus, Iran has been moving between 1.3 and 1.6 million barrels of crude per day through a ghost fleet of 400 to 430 dark tankers, routing them to Chinese teapot refineries in Shandong province, and settling the transactions primarily in yuan — not dollars.

At pre-war Brent prices in the low seventies, that shadow flow was generating somewhere between $35 billion and $45 billion in gross annual revenue. But here is the first structural asymmetry that explains everything: Iran has been selling those barrels at discounts of $8 to $13 per barrel below the ICE Brent benchmark to maintain Chinese demand and keep the sanctions-proof lifeline operational. Stack those discounts across the full volume over 2022 to 2025, and Iran has transferred an estimated $30 billion to $40 billion in foregone revenue to Beijing — an implicit subsidy that kept Chinese refiners cheap on energy while slowly starving the formal Iranian treasury.

From Washington's perspective, this arrangement is not simply about lost revenue for an adversary. It is a slow bleed of the petrodollar order itself. Every barrel settled in yuan through a front company in Shandong rather than in dollars through Dubai or London is one fewer unit of structural demand for US Treasuries, one more precedent for an energy architecture where the dollar is optional. The shadow channel had become too large, too institutionalized, and too strategically positioned at the mouth of the world's most critical chokepoint to ignore indefinitely.

The eight-body problem begins here. Once you accept this framing — a war not over oil but over the financial system that prices and settles oil — every subsequent move by every actor becomes rational. The apparent irrationality dissolves. What remains is a set of cold ledger calculations, executed under fire.

## II. Before the First Strike

The ten years prior to 28 February 2026 were not prelude. They were cause.

Iran's formal macroeconomic position entering the war was dire by any conventional measure. Inflation had exceeded forty percent for four consecutive years. The IMF's assessment of Iran's fiscal breakeven — the Brent price required to avoid a budget deficit — stood at approximately $121 per barrel in 2024, rising toward $124 in 2025. Those numbers do not incorporate the Chinese discount. Once you adjust for the effective $13 per barrel haircut that Iranian crude commands in sanctioned markets, the regime's real netback requirement — the price at which the IRGC patronage network, the Basij, and the clerical establishment can all be paid — was something in the range of the high nineties to low hundreds per barrel.

Against that breakeven, accessible foreign exchange reserves — the funds Iran could actually deploy, net of frozen accounts and sanctions frictions — stood at approximately $21 billion in 2023 and had risen to perhaps $24 to $26 billion by 2024. Total gross reserves were in the low thirties. The gap between those two figures represents Iran's debt to its own parallel system: money it technically holds but cannot freely spend. The accessible reserve position represented roughly an eighty percent collapse from pre-maximum-pressure levels.

The United States entered the conflict in a different kind of fiscal stress. General government debt stood near 123.8% of GDP. The federal deficit ran at approximately 5.9% of GDP in fiscal year 2025. The reserve currency issuer can always finance a war in its own currency — that constraint never binds for Washington — but the political economy of that financing, and its inflationary pass-through, absolutely does. For the United States, the binding constraint was never solvency. It was the oil-to-inflation-to-Treasury-yield transmission chain, and whether domestic politics would tolerate absorbing that shock.

Israel entered with considerably stronger formal buffers than its adversary. Foreign exchange reserves stood at $234.6 billion at end-February 2026 — 38.2% of GDP. Public debt was 68.6% of GDP at end-2025. Defense spending had been elevated above pre-conflict norms for over a year. The Israeli state was not wealthy enough to be indifferent to the war's cost, but it was capitalized well enough to sustain it for significantly longer than Iran.

Russia had been watching all of this. Every month of sustained oil above $90 per barrel was a fiscal lifeline for Moscow — Urals crude and Brent have historically tracked closely enough that the Gulf war premium was a direct subsidy to the Russian budget. And Russia, having built its own shadow-fleet architecture to survive Western sanctions after February 2022, had every incentive to see the Western sanctions toolkit further discredited.

Europe had no oil, no arms-export windfall, and no seat at any table where these decisions were being made. What it had was exposure — pure, undiversified, structural energy import dependence that the war would price with brutal efficiency.

The threshold crossing happened silently, across several numbers converging simultaneously: China's share of Iranian oil exports above eighty percent; the cumulative China discount approaching forty billion dollars; GCC sovereign wealth funds managing nearly six trillion dollars in overwhelmingly dollar-denominated assets; and Iran's fiscal breakeven projected above the pre-war oil price. At that point, the shadow channel was too large, too embedded, and too strategically placed to be ignored. The first strike was not an outbreak of violence. It was a forced repricing.

## III. The Eight Bodies

### Body One: Iran — The Asymmetric Levered State

Iran is the most misunderstood balance sheet in this conflict. Measured by GDP, military spending, or formal reserve levels, it is categorically weaker than every other actor in this analysis. SIPRI puts Iranian military expenditure at roughly $7.9 billion in 2024 — a fraction of what the US, Israel, or even the GCC states spend. Inflation has eroded purchasing power for four consecutive years. The formal economy is structurally impaired.

None of that is the correct measure of Iranian financial power in wartime.

The correct measure is the IRGC's parallel economy: the bonyad empire — with Setad alone estimated at approximately $95 billion in assets and Bonyad Mostazafan controlling some 160 corporate holdings across energy, finance, construction, and mining — plus the shadow-fleet operation, the proxy funding networks, and critically, the Hormuz optionality. Iran's binding constraint is not cash. It is purchasing power, hard-currency access, and the solvency of the patronage network that keeps the regime politically legitimate.

Iran's financial strategy is correspondingly asymmetric: impose cost rather than outspend. This is the logic of a state that knows it cannot win a symmetric balance-sheet contest and has therefore engineered an asymmetric one.

The Hormuz card is the clearest expression of this logic. Iran's own incremental loss from closing the strait is modest — its shadow fleet exports continue at reduced levels outside the main chokepoint, and official exports through conventional tankers were already negligible pre-war. The global cost, by contrast, is enormous: 20.9 million barrels per day repriced $30 to $50 higher, plus knock-on inflation, freight market seizure, and insurance market withdrawal. At peak war premium, the incremental daily cost to global energy importers runs north of a billion dollars. Iran absorbs a fraction of that. The ratio of external cost imposed to internal cost absorbed is the measure of Iran's real leverage — and it is an order of magnitude in Tehran's favour.

Iran's entry in this war's ledger also includes the proxy network. Annual funding to Hezbollah reached approximately $1 billion in 2025 despite domestic austerity. Houthi Red Sea toll operations were generating an estimated $180 million per month in covert shipping fees from vessels paying for safe passage. These are not charities. They are subsidiaries — self-financing nodes in a distributed deterrence architecture that imposes costs on shipping lanes from the Red Sea to the Gulf of Oman simultaneously.

### Body Two: The United States — The Financial Hegemon Playing Defense

The US is both the strongest actor at the table and the most constrained by what it stands to lose.

Washington can fund military operations in its own currency. That is the terminal advantage of reserve currency status. But the binding constraint — the real one — is the inflationary transmission chain. Oil shock → inflation expectations → Treasury yield spike → tighter financial conditions → political backlash. With general government debt already near 124% of GDP and a fiscal deficit running near 6%, the United States cannot absorb a sustained oil shock without consequence.

The US financial strategy in this conflict operates on three simultaneous tracks. The first is petrodollar defense: preserving the Gulf security architecture that keeps GCC producers pricing in dollars, pegging to the dollar, and recycling surpluses into Treasuries and US equities. GCC states hold an estimated $800 billion in FX reserves to sustain their dollar pegs, and their sovereign wealth funds manage approaching $6 trillion in overwhelmingly dollar-denominated assets. That is the structural buyer of last resort for US government debt. Anything that permanently redirects those flows — into yuan, into euros, into gold — is existentially important to Washington's ability to run the deficits it runs.

The second track is the war-premium windfall distribution. A Brent price sustained in the $90 to $120 range is a massive transfer from energy-importing economies to US shale producers and LNG exporters. Permian new-well breakeven sits near $61 per barrel; legacy wells in the mid-thirties. At $105 Brent — where the market was trading by March 26 — every marginal US barrel is throwing off pure economic rent. The same war that is inflicting consumer pain at the pump is simultaneously delivering exceptional cash flows to the US energy complex and its financiers.

The third track is the arms sales flywheel. Within three weeks of the conflict's start, Washington had approved or advanced somewhere between $16.5 billion and $23 billion in emergency arms packages to UAE, Kuwait, and Jordan — air defense radars, munitions, drones, F-16 upgrades — explicitly justified by Iranian strike capability and Gulf infrastructure vulnerability. These deals land on top of a pre-existing multi-year procurement pipeline worth tens of billions more.

Washington's dominant strategy in this conflict is controlled instability: degrade Iran's military capability and nuclear programme enough to send a clear message about the costs of yuan-denominated energy experimentation, but not so thoroughly as to implode GCC regimes or permanently close Hormuz. A normalized, sanctions-lifted Iran pumping 3 to 4 million barrels per day into global markets at standard commercial terms would be disastrous for US shale margins, Aramco's pricing power, and Washington's ability to weaponize the financial system. The revealed preference is: keep Iran wounded, not buried.

### Body Three: Israel — Buying Down the Tail Risk

Israel enters this conflict with no oil and no pretension of energy relevance. Its balance sheet is entirely about security cost of capital.

The Israeli defense complex — Elbit Systems, Israel Aerospace Industries, Rafael — had combined arms revenues in the range of $16.2 billion annually going into 2026, with Elbit alone carrying a market capitalization near $27.5 billion, the second-most valuable Israeli public company. War generates direct valuation upside for these firms: demonstrated demand for missile defense systems, UAVs, air defense radars, and precision munitions drives both export pipelines and domestic procurement simultaneously.

On the liability side, the financial logic runs the other direction. A credible Iranian nuclear capability raises Israel's sovereign risk premium — wider CDS spreads, higher government borrowing costs, elevated equity risk premia across the entire economy. Even a 50 to 100 basis point reduction in long-term funding costs, sustained over decades of borrowing on a $400 to $500 billion economy, has a net present value in the tens of billions. Israel is, in this frame, paying an elevated current premium — higher defense spending, disrupted economic activity, direct war costs — to buy down a much larger long-tail risk. That is the logic of insurance, not aggression.

Israel's binding constraint is not financial. It is domestic political tolerance for an extended conflict and the diplomatic management of an operation that requires US political cover to sustain. The FX reserves at $234.6 billion provide significant runway. The harder question is how long the political system can absorb the cost of a war whose financial benefits — reduced nuclear risk premium, defense complex valuation uplift — are diffuse and long-dated, while its costs are immediate and visible.

### Body Four: China — The Reluctant Short-Term Loser Playing a Long Game

China's position in this conflict is the most structurally interesting of the eight. It is simultaneously the actor with the most to lose in the short term and the most to gain across a five-to-ten year horizon.

The short-term arithmetic is unambiguous. China imports over 10 million barrels of crude per day. It was purchasing approximately 1.38 million barrels per day from Iran in 2025 — roughly 13 to 14% of its seaborne crude imports — at discounts of $8 to $13 per barrel. That discount disappears in a war that disrupts Iranian supply. More importantly, when Brent moves from the low seventies to $105 or above, the incremental cost to Chinese industry and consumers dwarfs any individual line item. China is the single largest loser from sustained high oil, in absolute dollar terms, among the eight actors in this analysis.

China's binding constraint is the import bill shock and the growth drag it feeds into an economy already managing a real estate debt overhang and a structural transition away from export-led growth. Beijing cannot absorb $120 oil indefinitely without visible economic consequence.

And yet. The long game looks completely different. A partially closed Hormuz, a discredited petrodollar system, and a wounded Iran that must sell even more of its oil to a single buyer at even deeper discounts opens possibilities that were previously theoretical. More oil trade in yuan — including potentially a yuan-cleared passage arrangement through Hormuz itself — would represent the most aggressive structural challenge to dollar hegemony since the original petrodollar bargain in the 1970s. Deeper integration of Iran as a Chinese client state, dependent on Beijing for finance, technology, and security guarantees, extends China's influence into the Gulf in ways that no amount of diplomatic maneuvering could achieve in peacetime.

China's dominant strategy is non-intervention with strategic hedging: absorb the short-term shock using strategic reserves and a diversified import slate drawing more heavily on Russian crude, while quietly institutionalizing any yuan-clearing arrangements that emerge from Hormuz bargaining. China does not want Iran to win outright — a normalized Iran would stop selling at a discount. It wants a perpetually wounded Iran that remains structurally dependent on a single buyer.

### Body Five: The GCC — Windfall, Vulnerability, and the Dollar Hostage

The Gulf Cooperation Council states occupy the most non-linear payoff function in this conflict. Their financial position swings from extraordinary windfall to structural catastrophe depending on a relatively narrow band of variables.

Saudi Aramco earned $106.2 billion in net income on $436 billion of revenue in 2024, with average Brent around $80 per barrel. Riyadh's fiscal breakeven is widely estimated near $90 per barrel. At sustained $110 to $120 Brent, the Saudi state runs fiscal surpluses that fund Vision 2030 mega-projects without debt expansion. Every dollar above breakeven at Aramco's production scale is transformative.

The constraint is physical. Roughly 20 million barrels per day flows through Hormuz under normal conditions. Saudi Arabia's East-West Petroline — designed for up to 7 million barrels per day — is realistically moving 3 to 5 million barrels per day to Yanbu under wartime operational constraints. The UAE's Habshan-Fujairah pipeline can handle approximately 1.5 to 1.8 million barrels per day. Combined bypass capacity is somewhere between 4.7 and 7 million barrels per day — roughly 13 to 30% of normal Hormuz throughput. The gap between what the GCC can export when the strait is disrupted and what it could export under normal conditions is the measure of its physical vulnerability.

Then there is Qatar. Iranian strikes on Ras Laffan in March destroyed approximately 17% of Qatar's LNG export capacity, triggering long-term force majeure declarations and wiping out an estimated $20 billion of annual revenue for a period that will be measured in years, not months. That is a concentrated, permanent-seeming loss imposed on one of the strait's critical nodes.

Beneath all of this sits the financial hostage dynamic. GCC sovereign wealth funds manage approaching $6 trillion in assets: ADIA near $993 billion, PIF around $925 billion, QIA in the hundreds of billions. These portfolios are overwhelmingly dollar-denominated — US Treasuries, US and European equities, private equity, infrastructure. The dollar assets are the quiet collateral in the US-GCC security bargain. If the petrodollar system fractures and Gulf states pivot aggressively into yuan or euro assets, they crystallize foreign exchange losses and sacrifice the implicit US sanctions protection their dollar positioning provides. That mutual dependence is what keeps Riyadh and Abu Dhabi from acting on their BRICS and yuan-settlement flirtations, even as the war exposes the limits of their Washington relationship.

### Body Six: Russia — The Sanctioned Beneficiary

Russia is the conflict's quietest winner.

Every dollar that Brent rises above the pre-war level of the low seventies is a direct fiscal transfer to the Russian state. The Russian budget has been structured around oil and gas revenues for decades; sanctions-era adaptations shifted the pricing mechanism and some of the routing, but not the fundamental dependency. At sustained $100 to $120 Brent, Moscow runs surpluses that fund the Ukraine campaign without requiring the kind of emergency fiscal maneuvers that mid-cycle oil prices had necessitated.

Russia is also a template for Iran's endurance strategy. The shadow fleet Iran operates was built partly on lessons learned from Russia's post-2022 evasion architecture — the same ship-to-ship transfer mechanics, the same flag-of-convenience registrations, the same network of intermediary buyers. The two sanctioned states share infrastructure, trading houses, and evasion playbooks in ways that Western designations have repeatedly documented without successfully dismantling.

Beyond the price windfall, the Iran war serves Russian strategic interests by consuming Western military attention, straining US alliances across multiple theaters simultaneously, and further testing the credibility of the sanctions toolkit that Washington has been deploying against Moscow for four years. Each instance of sanctions proving insufficient to change state behavior weakens the deterrent value of the next round. Russia has every incentive to see that process accelerate.

Russia's role in this conflict is largely passive — beneficiary rather than participant. It does not need to act to profit. The eight-body problem runs the trajectory for it.

### Body Seven: Europe — The Unrepresented Creditor

Europe is the starkest case study in what it means to absorb costs from decisions made entirely by others.

There is no European shale offset. The continent imports the overwhelming majority of its crude and LNG. When Brent moves from $70 to $105, that differential lands directly on European industrial energy costs — on German manufacturing, on French utilities, on the energy-intensive sectors that were already under structural pressure from the post-2021 European energy crisis. The deindustrialisation dynamic that had been playing out slowly since Russia's 2022 gas cutoff now receives an acute overlay.

Europe has no seat at any table where the decisions governing this conflict are being made. It is not party to the US-Israel military planning. It has no meaningful leverage over Iranian decision-making. It cannot influence Chinese strategic hedging. What it can do is absorb the inflationary transmission and watch its current account deteriorate.

The European Central Bank finds itself in a position of impossible simultaneity: an oil-shock-driven inflation impulse arguing for tighter policy, colliding with the growth drag from that same oil shock arguing for accommodation. The two forces do not resolve cleanly. The policy path runs through genuine uncertainty.

Europe's long-run exposure here is also monetary. If the war accelerates yuan-settlement experiments and chips further at dollar hegemony, the euro does not replace the dollar as the beneficiary. The shift, if it happens, flows toward yuan-denominated mechanisms — further marginalizing European financial infrastructure in the global commodity-settlement architecture.

### Body Eight: Turkey and UAE — The Corridor States

Turkey and the UAE are categorically different from the other six actors: they are not principals in the conflict. They are the infrastructure through which money moves regardless of what the principals decide.

Dubai's free zones, gold markets, cryptocurrency exchanges, and trade-finance ecosystem have been documented across multiple sanctions investigations as the primary pressure valve for Iran's parallel economy. Iran-UAE total trade — formal and informal combined — exceeded $27 billion annually by 2025, making the UAE Iran's second-largest commercial partner after China. Front companies in Dubai re-label Iranian non-oil goods, facilitate petrochemical re-exports, and provide access to offshore dollars through intermediaries who price in a significant risk premium at every conversion step.

Turkey operates analogously through Istanbul: providing exchange houses, trade-finance access, and re-export routing that allows Iranian commercial relationships to breathe through the sanctions cordon. The Istanbul corridor has been particularly important for non-oil trade — construction materials, industrial equipment, consumer goods — that the formal Chinese relationship cannot efficiently supply.

Both corridor states are in a delicate position as the war intensifies. Higher sanctions enforcement pressure and secondary sanctions risk threaten the profitability of the intermediary business. At the same time, the war premium on that business — the risk margin that intermediaries charge Iran for access to dollar-clearing and re-export infrastructure — rises simultaneously with the enforcement pressure. The net position is ambiguous. What is not ambiguous is that both states will continue to operate the corridor as long as the economics justify it, because the alternative is forfeiting a multi-decade business model that has become structurally embedded in their financial systems.

## IV. The Chokepoint

The mathematics of Hormuz are simple and devastating.

In the first half of 2025, the strait handled 20.9 million barrels per day of crude and petroleum products, plus 11.4 billion cubic feet per day of LNG — approximately one-fifth of global traded oil and a comparable fraction of global LNG. Identified bypass infrastructure — Saudi Arabia's East-West Petroline and the UAE's Habshan-Fujairah pipeline — can move a combined 4.7 million barrels per day under realistic wartime operating conditions. The unbypassable gap is 16.2 million barrels per day.

Iran does not need to perfectly close the strait to create systemic leverage. It needs only to demonstrate enough disruption risk that insurers withdraw cover, shippers reroute or stand down, refiners draw on emergency stocks, and central banks begin pricing in a persistent inflation shock. Commercial viability fails before navies formally close anything. The insurance market is a harder variable than the military one: when war-risk premiums spike by more than 1,000% on some Gulf shipping routes — as they reportedly did in early March — the economic closure of Hormuz precedes the physical one.

The IEA's response was a coordinated 400 million barrel emergency release — the largest in the organisation's history. That is roughly nineteen days of Hormuz-equivalent throughput, or somewhat less when adjusted for the crude quality and logistics constraints on emergency stock deployment. It buys time. It does not resolve the structural exposure.

The chokepoint's financial potency derives from precisely this gap between physical bypass capacity and normal throughput. Iran does not need to sink tankers to impose a trillion-dollar-scale disruption. It needs only to make the risk of transiting the strait expensive enough that the global logistics system routes around it — and the routing-around costs, in time, fuel, insurance, and capital, are passed through into every energy price in every economy on earth.

## V. The Hidden Money

The visible war has a balance sheet. The invisible one has a nervous system.

The IRGC's sanctions-evasion architecture is the most important financial infrastructure in this conflict that receives almost no serious coverage in mainstream financial media. The basic mechanics are documented across OFAC designation records, the GMU TRACCC Dubai report, FDD analysis, and multiple rounds of secondary sanctions enforcement.

Crude loads at Kharg Island and smaller Gulf terminals. A fleet of 400 to 430 dark tankers — registered through shell companies in Hong Kong, Dubai, Panama, and the Marshall Islands — conducts ship-to-ship transfers in international waters, typically off Malaysia, in the Gulf of Oman, or under false Malaysian or other third-country origin documentation. The cargo arrives at teapot refineries in Shandong, optimized over years to process discounted Iranian heavy crude efficiently. Settlement flows into front accounts and trading houses linked to IRGC-aligned networks, denominated in yuan.

Iran cannot freely convert those yuan holdings into hard currency. Sanctions and correspondent banking risk make large-block conversion impossible through conventional channels. So the yuan circulates through three mechanisms: payment for Chinese goods and services — often overpriced, with front companies taking a margin on the inflation; conversion into gold, cryptocurrency, or offshore dollars through Dubai, Hong Kong, and Istanbul intermediaries at significant risk premiums; and in some portion, simply left as a de facto line of credit to Beijing, whose buyers benefit from the arrangement without having to formalize it.

Every conversion step is a tax on Iran and a subsidy to the intermediary network. Sanctions have not stopped the flows. They have restructured them into a hierarchy where IRGC entities, Chinese refiners, and corridor-state intermediaries skim the margin, and the formal Iranian treasury receives whatever is left after the network's toll.

The IRGC's bonyad system functions as a sovereign wealth fund for the regime's parallel state: asset-rich, legally structured as non-governmental charitable foundations, partially insulated from formal sanctions because they are not sovereign entities, and able to interface with global finance through subsidiaries, joint ventures, and real estate positions in jurisdictions that maintain commercial relationships with Iran. Setad's estimated $95 billion asset base. Bonyad Mostazafan's 160 operating subsidiaries. These are not adjuncts to the Iranian state. They are the financial state — the part that survives regardless of what happens to the formal government.

The Houthi revenue model deserves separate attention for what it reveals about the proxy network's financial sophistication. A UN-linked assessment in late 2024 estimated that Houthi "safe transit fees" — covertly charged to shipowners seeking to avoid Red Sea attacks — ran approximately $180 million per month, over $2 billion annually. That is a self-financing toll booth on a strategic artery, generating revenue from the very disruption it creates. From Tehran's perspective, the Houthis are not a dependent subsidiary. They are a self-funding node that imposes costs on global shipping without drawing on Iranian fiscal capacity.

## VI. The Four-Day Delay

This is the detail that reveals most clearly what kind of actor Iran actually is.

The war begins on 28 February 2026. Brent, trading in the low seventies pre-conflict, moves into the mid-to-high eighties by 1 to 3 March as markets price geopolitical risk but Hormuz continues to function. Traffic slows. Risk premiums rise. But the strait has not closed.

Full effective closure — tanker traffic reduced to a trickle, insurance markets effectively non-functional, the IEA triggering emergency stock releases — happens around 8 to 9 March, ten days into the conflict. Brent touches near $120 to $126 intraday at peak, before settling into a $100 to $110 range as emergency releases provide partial relief.

The delay is not indecision. It is an options strategy.

By waiting, Iran allowed the war-risk premium to build in the market before playing the full Hormuz card. The marginal shock value of the formal closure, when it came, was maximized because markets had already priced in a significant but incomplete risk. The stair-step price structure — initial spike to the eighties, then a parabolic move to near $120 on actual closure — is the signature of an actor who was watching the tape, monitoring how much risk premium markets were assigning per unit of escalation, and timing the full card to land when the marginal disruption value per day was at its highest.

Tehran was simultaneously calculating how much of its own shadow-fleet capacity it could re-route outside the main chokepoint before closing the door on everyone else. The four-day gap between the first strike and the full closure is the time required to answer that question internally.

In game-theoretic terms, this is delayed brinkmanship — escalate only after observing that the adversary's marginal cost per additional week of conflict exceeds your own. The revealed strategy is not emotional response. It is the behavior of an actor whose objective function is to weaponize global price levels, and who understands option timing well enough to execute accordingly.

## VII. Three Outcomes

The eight-body problem does not have one solution. It has a probability distribution across three structurally distinct outcomes, each with different financial market implications.

### Outcome A — Managed De-escalation with Partial Reopening

Probability: Medium. The most politically convenient outcome for Washington — enough military damage to Iran to declare deterrence restored, enough diplomatic architecture to partially reopen Hormuz before the oil shock becomes politically toxic domestically. Brent retreats from shock highs into the high eighties to low nineties. Insurance markets gradually improve. Emergency stock releases buy time for a negotiated passage arrangement.

The market signature is a rapid rotation out of the commodities and energy complex into cyclicals and duration. The risk premium embedded in Brent front-month contracts — which had been running $20 to $30 above what supply-demand fundamentals alone would justify — deflates over weeks.

What this outcome does not resolve: the shadow oil channel, the IRGC's bonyad architecture, and the yuan-settlement experiments. The parallel financial state survives. The war resets the military balance at the margin but leaves the structural financial conflict entirely intact. The shadow channel goes underground, adapts its routing, and resumes — probably within six months.

### Outcome B — Prolonged Semi-Closure and Intermittent Disruption

Probability: High. The most consistent outcome with current evidence. Hormuz alternates between partial and full disruption for six to twelve months. Brent sustains in the $100 to $130 range. LNG benchmarks remain elevated. Shipping insurance markets function at dramatically higher premium levels that effectively price out marginal voyages. The IEA exhausts and then partially replenishes emergency stocks.

This is the outcome where the cross-asset divergence becomes most pronounced. US shale E&Ps with unhedged production run super-cycle margins. LNG exporters — Cheniere, the portfolio of US Gulf Coast terminals — see export economics that justify accelerated capital expenditure and long-term contract premiums. US and Israeli defense firms book multi-year order pipelines. GCC sovereign wealth funds accumulate inflows at elevated prices, even as physical export volumes are constrained by the bypass capacity gap.

The losers in this scenario are structurally clear. European industrial competitiveness — already damaged by the 2021 to 2022 energy crisis and its aftermath — sustains another acute leg lower. German chemical and manufacturing sectors face energy costs that make production economics increasingly questionable relative to US competitors. South and Southeast Asian economies face balance-of-payments stress as their energy import bills balloon in local currency terms while their export revenues stagnate. EM foreign exchange markets come under sustained pressure.

The monetary policy implication is most complex for the Federal Reserve. A sustained oil shock is simultaneously inflationary — justifying tighter policy — and recessionary in its demand-destruction effects, particularly on lower-income households whose consumption baskets are disproportionately energy-intensive. The Fed has no clean policy response. It manages the tension between the two until one force clearly dominates.

This outcome is also where the yuan-Hormuz story becomes most consequential. If Iran — formally or through intermediaries — offers preferential passage to tankers settling in yuan while disrupting dollar-cleared cargoes, that is not a curiosity. It is the most direct challenge to petrodollar architecture since OPEC 1973. The arrangement would create a revealed price for yuan-denominated energy access, institutionalize non-dollar settlement in a chokepoint through which a fifth of global oil flows, and establish a precedent that every subsequent energy geopolitical crisis will reference. The reason this possibility receives so little mainstream financial coverage is precisely its threat level: US and GCC policymakers have no interest in advertising the alternative, and China benefits from a quiet transition rather than a dramatic one.

### Outcome C — Negotiated Settlement, Regime Survives in Diminished Form

Probability: Medium-Low in the near term, rising over a twelve-to-eighteen month horizon. Some form of negotiated arrangement — nuclear concessions exchanged for partial sanctions relief, Hormuz formally reopened under monitored conditions — brings Brent back toward an $85 to $95 equilibrium.

The financial market reading of this outcome is nuanced. Oil falls far enough from war-premium highs to provide consumer relief, but not far enough to damage US shale economics severely. The IRGC's bonyad network survives — moves further underground, upgrades its evasion methodology based on wartime operational lessons, and resumes discounted oil flows to China within a restructured routing architecture. The parallel financial state does not dismantle. It adapts.

China entrenches as Iran's indispensable patron: the creditor, the technology supplier, the single buyer of last resort. The dependency deepens. The yuan-settlement proportion of Iranian trade flows stays elevated or rises. The structural erosion of petrodollar dominance continues, slower and quieter than in Outcome B, but directionally unchanged.

This is the sanctions-as-business-model outcome. The war does not resolve the underlying financial architecture conflict. It reprices it.

## VIII. What Comes Next

The one financial fact that explains this entire conflict is the following: for a decade, Iran transferred tens of billions of dollars of oil value out of the petrodollar system and into a discounted, yuan-linked shadow channel that subsidized Beijing's energy bill and starved Washington's leverage. The war happens because that channel became too large, too embedded, and too strategically positioned to ignore.

Once you hold that fact clearly, the apparent chaos of eight actors pursuing seemingly contradictory objectives resolves into something coherent. Iran weaponizes the chokepoint because its fiscal breakeven exceeds the pre-war oil price and its only real deterrent is to make adversary pain exceed its own. The United States pursues controlled instability because a normalized Iran would destroy shale margins, reduce arms-sales demand, and undermine the petrodollar architecture that funds American deficits. China hedges because it loses in the short term and wins in the long term. Russia collects the windfall without firing a shot. Europe pays the bill without a voice.

The eight-body problem has no stable equilibrium. Orbital mechanics teaches that eight bodies of sufficiently different mass, each exerting gravitational force on the others, will produce trajectories that are sensitive to initial conditions and effectively impossible to predict past a certain time horizon. Small perturbations — a successful Iranian strike on a Fujairah terminal, a US Treasury action designating a major Chinese teapot refinery, an Israeli surgical strike that triggers a wider escalation — can shift the probability distribution between outcomes dramatically.

What markets can price, and what this analysis attempts to map, is the structural logic underneath the chaos: who holds what, what each actor's binding constraint actually is, where the money flows when the formal system is bypassed, and what the three broad destination states look like in financial terms.

The strait is twenty-one miles wide at its narrowest. Through that channel flows not just crude oil and liquefied gas, but the tension between two incompatible financial architectures — one denominated in dollars, anchored in Treasuries, maintained by GCC peg commitments; the other denominated in yuan, settled through ghost fleets and teapot refineries, financed by discount transfers and bonyad conglomerates. The war is the market forcing a reconciliation between those two systems.

The eight bodies are still in motion. The problem remains unsolved.
